Grammar and Writing


OWL: Purdue's Online Writing Lab

 (Copyright ©1995-2003 by OWL at Purdue University and Purdue University. All rights reserved.)

Purdue's OWL is perhaps the oldest and most well respected online writing lab. The site offers help with the mechanics of writing. Though developed for university students, the writing guides are useful for writers at all levels. See the "Handouts and Materials" section, especially the area on "General Writing Concerns" (broken into the subheadings "Effective Writing," "Revising/Editing/Proofreading," and "Types/Genres of Writing") and "Grammar, spelling, and punctuation" (which includes grammar exercises).

Guide to Grammar and Writing

 (Copyright 2004, Professor Charles Darling and the Capital Community College Foundation, Hartford, CT )

A great place for answers to your grammar questions, with sub-divisions for questions at the "word & sentence level," "paragraph level," and "essay & research paper level." This site also includes 170+ grammar quizzes. Yay?

HyperGrammar

 (Written by David Megginson (editor). Copyright © 1994, 1995 and 1996 by the University of Ottawa )

A very basic (basic is sometimes good!) grammar from the University of Ottawa. All descriptions and examples are clear and concise; I especially like the easy-to-search "Definitions" section.

Modern English Grammar

 (© 1995, 2004 Daniel Kies. All rights reserved.)

An exhaustive online grammar created by Daniel Kies, professor of English at the College of DePage (IL). Provides easy to follow descriptions and examples of everything from "Direct Objects" to "Principles of Syntax."

Confusing Words

 (Copyright ©2004 Confusing Words. Created by Richard and Anne Wanderman)

As the website states, "Confusing Words is a collection of 3210 words that are troublesome to readers and writers. Words are grouped according to the way they are most often confused or misused. Some of these words are homonyms (words that sound alike but are spelled differently) and some are just commonly confused."

"Resources for Writers"

 (This page is maintained by Jack Lynch.)

A collection of writing and grammar links, put together by Jack Lynch, professor of English at Rutgers University, Newark campus, and located on his Literary Resources on the Net site.


Rhetoric

Rhetoric is the art of persuasion in public speaking. In short, rhetoric offers tools and effective strategies for coordinating what we say with how we say it. Formal rhetoric instruction dates back to Greek and Roman times. In the nineteenth century, notably at Harvard University, classical rhetoric was employed for the teaching of writing. Today, though instruction in rhetoric has generally fallen out of favor, some university and college writing programs, including Stanford University's, have again turned to rhetorical models of persuasion to help students form strong written arguments.

Silva Rhetoricae: The Forest of Rhetoric

 (© 1996-2003, Gideon O. Burton, Brigham Young University.)

This site offers a fantastic introduction to all aspects of rhetoric, including descriptions of its component parts, explanations of the styles of rhetorical arguments, definitions of key rhetorical terms, or "figures," (such as, "metaphor," "synechdoche," and "apostrophe"), and concrete examples of these styles and terms put into practice.
